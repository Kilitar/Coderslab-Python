# History of databases

Type: Article

## Short history of databases...

> **Data** -  everything what is/can be processed/transformed in computational and mental processes.

The definition is quoted from "Global TOGA Meta-Theory" by Adam Gadomski.

In this sense, data is a relative concept, existing only together with the concept of data processing, and can take such forms as:

- signs,
- speech,
- graphs,
- signals.

Different data can provide the same information, but the same data can also provide different information.

On the other hand: e.g., collections of numbers or words can be data, but if we do not know what they represent, they are not information.

## Automation of processing

- People began making the first tools about 3.4 million years ago.
- Writing has been used to encode data for several thousand years.
- The first tool processing punched card data to automate work was a loom built in 1908.
- Special punch cards were used in 1890 for the US census.
- In 1928, machines and cards acquired a general purpose and were standardized; magnetic tapes appeared.
- In 1944, the first calculations were made with ENIAC - Electronic Numerical Integrator And Computer.
- In 1956, IBM built the first 5 MB hard drive.
- As early as 1959, the CODASYL ("Conference on Data Systems Languages") consortium was formed to oversee and stimulate the development of standards for data description and manipulation languages.

## The 70s

- Codd created a relational database model.
- Chamberlin and Boyce at IBM developed the SEQUEL language (Structure English Query Language) later renamed SQL.
- Ellison - founder of Relational Software (renamed ORACLE 6 years later) together with Miner and Oates created the first commercial relational database ORACLE.
- Wurtz and Karrash initiated the foundations of the theory of multidimensional databases.

## The 80s (standardization of SQL)

After not very successful attempts to create the original object-oriented databases, the object model was later implemented in relational databases.

ANSI published the `SQL` language standard, later accepted by ISO.

Teradata created parallel databases for data warehouses.

## The 90s - creation of `OLAP` and `MySQL`

- The work of theorists under Codd's leadership was already moving towards multidimensional data integration.
- The MySQL database management system was developed in Sweden.
- XML (Extensible Markup Language) was used for online access to databases.

## Early 21st century - creation of non-relational databases

- Bitcoin is created (2008).
- Key-value databases (dictionaries, hash tables, associative arrays). Redis is created.
- Document databases (mainly with documents in JSON and XML formats). MongoDB is created.
- Graph databases (based on nodes, edges and properties). Neo4J is created.
- Wide column databases (like relational, but without a rigid schema). Google Bigtable is created.
- Distributed databases (functioning despite the failure of one of the servers). Cassandra is created.

## What will the future bring?

Most likely moving to the cloud and using artificial intelligence...

It's hard to make predictions in the field of databases, but observing the latest trends, we can assume that:

- Despite the growth of `NoSQL`, it will not replace `SQL` because it sacrifices data compliance for performance.
- Artificial intelligence as well as the data itself will be available in clouds for convenient scaling. AI will be involved in index optimization, self-security, self-correction.

## Summary

This article familiarized us with how databases have evolved: we know that the first database systems were developed as early as the 1970s.

Admittedly, it is hard to predict what the future will look like, but at this point we expect that `SQL` databases will not lose their popularity.
