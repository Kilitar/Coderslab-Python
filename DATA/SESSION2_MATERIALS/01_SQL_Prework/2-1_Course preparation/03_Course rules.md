# Course rules

Type: Article

In the previous article we have discussed the structure of our course. It is time to get familiar with the rules to follow during the course. We divided them into several parts, including:

1. Communication during the course,
2. Rules of completing the prework,
3. Rules of participating in the classes.

## Contacting people involved in the course

During the course you are going to meet: the course specialist, mentor, and lecturer. Understanding their scopes of responsibilities and knowing how to communicate with them is going to pay off.

### Course specialist

Each Coders Lab course has its specialist assigned. This is a person you have already met. Course specialist is responsible for the schedule, contracts, payments and course organization. Consider the course specialist the "guiding spirit" of the course: if you run into any organizational problems the specialist should be the first person you turn to.

It is best to handle all matters with the course specialist via email.

### Mentor

In our course you have access to a mentor who will watch over you and all the people in the group throughout the course. You'll meet soon, during the welcome session. The mentor is responsible for the group in the content aspects of the course. This is the person you can write to if you encounter problems with an exercise, your computer, etc. Of course, try to solve the problem yourself first: this way, you will learn the most. If you are stuck and you really need help, go ahead: **write to your mentor**!

The main method of contacting the mentor is Slack. Your mentor or course specialist sent you the access to the app in the initial email.

Just keep in mind that your mentor has around 48 hours to answer. Usually this happens faster, but the reply may come the next day.

### Lecturer

Lecturer is the person responsible for teaching the knowledge from a session. Individual sessions may have different lecturers – not necessarily the mentor of your course. Our goal is to find the best people for each part of the course.

Each lecturer has access to Slack, and questions related to a particular module should be directed to the lecturer who teaches it. They will be able to respond immediately or raise the subject before the next class.

### Communication tools

If you do not already know the tools that we use for communication and teaching at Coders Lab, it is a good idea to get familiar with them. Mostly, we are going to use two programs:

1. Slack — a text-message communicator providing both subject-oriented channels and messages restricted to a selected group of people. [Slack website](https://slack.com/intl/en-pl/resources/using-slack/how-to-use-slack) offers basic instructions on how to use it.
2. Zoom — a video conferencing platform. It provides many activities useful when learning remotely, including: screen sharing, whiteboard, and 1:1 private rooms.

Get familiar with the programs used during the course: this way you will not feel lost during the first class.

## Prework

**Prework has to be completed before the start of the course!** Why is it so important? We have noticed a close correlation between engagement in prework and progress in the main part of the course. People who have taken the prework seriously tend to do much better in the course – the knowledge gained while solving exercises and reading articles prepared for this stage of the course is the basis for acquiring further skills.

**Remember to systematically complete the prework material!** Start working on your own as soon as possible to better acquire the material that is new to you. **Schedule time for it and don't put off work until the last minute.**

**Use the "Done" and "Read " buttons** at the very bottom of each section of the materials to monitor your progress in completing tasks and reading articles and presentations.

## Rules of participating in online classes

"SQL - Data Analysis" is a fully remote course. This means that you will be learning in the so-called "Virtual classroom". We use the [Zoom](https://zoom.us/) program to connect to class. Remember to install it before the first class. You are going to get the link to connect to the class from your course specialist.

Virtual classroom activities require more focus than on-site classes. That is why you need to remember about the online meetings rules:

1. Keep your camera on throughout the class — thanks to this the lecturer will know whether every class participant understands the subject or wants to ask more questions. Additionally, it is much easier to teach if you see the people you talk to.
2. Do not come late to class — this will let the whole group to best use the available time. The same applies to returning from breaks.
3. Before the class, organize your space so that you can stay focused on the course. The space should be quiet, well-lit and free from distractions as much as possible (nobody should disturb you during classes).
4. Mute your microphone if you are not talking at the moment — this will help to avoid echo and other noise that can disturb others in the group.
