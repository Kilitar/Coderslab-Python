# Motivation for choosing the tool used in the course

Type: Article

We decided to teach this course using a spreadsheet: Microsoft Excel 365 software in particular. Below you are going to learn why.

We chose spreadsheets because:

- they do not require programming skills,
- they are still the most popular tool for viewing and initial analysis of data,
- they naturally introduce thinking about data in tabular form; and they also allow you to go beyond that,
- they can give a good glimpse into what programming is,
- they are made for data analysis - preliminary and advanced,
- it's very easy to change data in them while working.

And we will use Microsoft Excel 365 because:

- it is still the most widespread and mature spreadsheet on the market,
- knowing the advanced functions of Excel, you'll be able to easily switch to other software, for example: Open Office or Google Sheets,
- installing the software is easy and we will provide licenses for the duration of the course,
- is very well integrated with many tools, not only those of Microsoft brand.

MS Excel has become a model and has lived to see many alternatives that replicate and mimic its operation. Unfortunately, we cannot guarantee that examples of functions, formulas or algebraic expressions will work correctly in alternative spreadsheets.

Excel itself has also changed dynamically over the past 20 years, and parts of the functionality and some formulas presented in this course are not present in its previous versions.

In this course we will be using MS Excel 365 in the original **English language version**. This is important because localizing the language settings changes the formulas and the way numbers and dates are formatted. One of the common issues when working with data includes details such as decimal separator (`,` vs `.` ) or date format, resulting directly from the local settings but quite difficult to eliminate.

However, where possible or necessary, descriptive explanations of functions will also be provided.

## Advantages and disadvantages of working with Excel

### Advantages

1. **Flexibility of editing data** – the greatest and undisputed advantage of Excel is how quickly we can open any collection of data for editing and enrich it with data from other files or even websites by copying it with the user-friendly keyboard shortcuts: `Ctrl+C` and `Ctrl+V` or the right-mouse-button menu.
2. **Ease of performing basic operations** - once the data is loaded, you can instantly move on to all kinds of data manipulation: from manually correcting values, formatting data in tables, documenting and organizing, to summaries, charts and complex mathematical operations.
  
  You'll quickly see that once you open a data file, all the basic and moderately advanced operations you'd like to perform with a set of data can be done in Excel in 3-4 clicks.
3. **Capacity** - Excel 365 can fit just over 1 million rows and 16000 columns in a single sheet. This is a figure that allows you to analyze the data you may encounter in most small businesses.
  
  However, practice shows that Excel ceases to be convenient when analyzing large files over 100 MB or several hundred thousand rows. The smooth flow of work will also depend on the complexity of the calculations being carried out and the computing power and RAM of the computer.

### Disadvantages

1. **Development and Maintenance** - as data volume grows, the freedom to manage entries into such a large file becomes an obstacle to developing and maintaining repeatable analysis. Adding more rows and columns can begin to interfere with previously created calculation fields.
  As calculations in the spreadsheet become more complex, their clarity and efficiency diminish; it's easy to make mistakes in data references. Often, too, once-created calculations are not versatile enough and, as the amount of data grows, they stop working correctly.
2. **Updating data** - over time, the problem that keeps appearing is that to update the data and deliver it to the right people, we need to open the file from time to time and perform a few simple operations to make it up-to-date again. If there are a lot of such analytical files, it can very quickly turn out that our main job is to open and update Excel files with analyses that we (or someone before us) have done (but they're still useful and need to be refreshed frequently).
3. **Limited automation** - Excel is becoming more and more convenient as a tool for linking to data, and you no longer need to *paste* the contents of files between them. Excel even allows direct linking to databases. However, when the frequency of updating data in Excel files becomes a challenge, this is the moment to convince your supervisors to invest in databases and solutions for automating the processing and presentation of information. Such examples could be business intelligence solutions or applications written in Python, accessible from a browser.
4. **Quality of charts** - It also turns out that the data visualizations that Excel offers are not as clear as in specialized Business Intelligence tools or dedicated libraries for scientific data visualization. Formatting a good chart in Excel proves to be time-consuming over time - especially initially, when software tools may require a bit more involvement in the creation of the first chart. Creating subsequent chart families can be very simple later on, and definitely faster than replicating the process manually in Excel.
