# Course rules

Type: Article

In the previous article we have discussed the structure of our course. It is time to get familiar with the rules to follow during the course. We divided them into several parts:

1. communication during the course,
2. rules of completing the *prework*,
3. rules of class participation.

## Contacting people involved in the course

During the course you are going to meet: the course specialist, mentor, and lecturer. Understanding their scopes of responsibilities and knowing how to communicate with them is going to pay off.

### Course specialist

In case of any questions regarding the organization of the course, you will get help from the course specialist. Each Coders Lab course has its specialist assigned. Course specialist is responsible for the schedule, contracts, payments and all **organization matters**. Consider the course specialist a "guiding spirit" of the course: if you run into any organizational problems the specialist should be the first person you turn to.

It is best to handle all matters with the course specialist via email — this will ensure that all your questions and specialist's answers are going to be in one place and it will be easier to come back to them as needed.

The course tutor will help you with an MS Office 365 license and possible access to the Excel basics course.

### Mentor

In our course you have your mentor who will watch over you and all the people in your group throughout the course. You will meet soon during the welcome session The mentor is responsible for the group in the content aspects of the course. They share their knowledge and provide direction when doubts arise. This is the person you can write to if you encounter problems with an exercise, your computer, etc. First, try to solve the problem yourself - this way, you will learn the most. But if you're stuck and really need help, go ahead: **write to your mentor!*

How to write to the mentor? The main method of contacting the mentor is Slack. Your mentor or course specialist sent you the access to the app in the initial email.

Just keep in mind that your mentor will most likely not respond immediately – they have around 48 hours to answer. Usually this happens faster, but the reply may also come the next day.

### Lecturer

Lecturer is the person responsible for teaching the knowledge from a specific session. Individual sessions may have different lecturers – not necessarily the mentor of your course. Our goal is to find the best people for each part of the course.

Each lecturer has access to Slack and questions related to a particular module should be directed to the lecturer who taught it. They will be able to respond immediately or address the subject before the next class.

## Communication tools

If you do not already know the tools that we use for communication and teaching at Coders Lab it is a good idea to get familiar with them. You will mostly use two programs:

1. **Slack** — is a text-message communicator providing both subject-oriented channels and messages private to a selected group of people. Slack [official website](https://slack.com/intl/en-pl/resources/using-slack/how-to-use-slack) offers basics instructions on how to use it.
2. **Zoom** — is a videoconferencing platform. Beyond that, it provides many activities useful when learning remotely, including: screen sharing, whiteboard, and 1:1 private rooms.
  
  Get familiar with the programs used during the course: this way you won't feel lost during the first class.

## Prework

Prework has to be completed before the start of the course! Why is it so important? We have noticed a great correlation between engagement in *prework* and progress in the main part of the course People who have taken the *prework* seriously tend to do much better in the course – the knowledge gained from solving exercises and reading articles prepared for this stage of the course is the basis for acquiring further skills.

Systematical progress in prework is the key to acquiring the new materials successfully!
**Schedule time for it and don't put off work until the last minute.**

> You can track your progress in the materials easily with the **"Read/Done"** buttons.

## Rules of participating in online classes

"Python - Data Analysis" is a fully remote course. This means that you'll be learning in the so-called "Virtual classroom". We use popular [Zoom](https://zoom.us/) program to connect to class. Remember: install it before the first class. You are going to get the link to connect to the virtual classroom from your course specialist.

Due to their format, virtual classroom activities require more focus than on-site classes. That is why you need to remember about a kind of savoir-vivre during the online meetings:

1. **Keep your camera on throughout the class** — thanks to this the lecturer will know whether every class participant understands the subject or wants to ask more questions. It is much easier to teach if you see the people you talk to.
2. **Do not be late to class** — this will let the whole group to best use the available time. The same applies to returning from breaks. Latecomers distract others.
3. **Before classes organize your space so that you can be fully focused on learning.** The space should be free from noise, well-lit and free from distractions as much as possible (nobody should disturb you during classes). If you are not alone remember to use headphones.
4. **Mute your microphone if you are not talking at the moment** — this will help to avoid echo and other noise that can disturb others in the group.
